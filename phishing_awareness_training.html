Phishing Awareness Training

Learn how to spot phishing emails, fake websites, and social engineering attacks.

What is Phishing?

Phishing is a cyber attack that tries to trick you into sharing sensitive information or clicking a malicious link. Attackers often use email, text messages, instant messages, or fake websites to appear legitimate.

Successful phishing attacks exploit trust, urgency, curiosity, or fear — not technical vulnerabilities.

How to Recognize Phishing Emails

  • Suspicious sender address: The display name may look real, but the email address is slightly wrong.
  • Urgency: Messages that push you to "act now" or "verify immediately" are red flags.
  • Unexpected attachments or links: Be cautious with any link or attachment you did not request.
  • Poor grammar or spelling: Legitimate organizations rarely send poorly written messages.
  • Requests for credentials: Reputable services will not ask you to reply with passwords or multi-factor authentication codes.

Example: Fake account alert

"Your account will be suspended unless you click here and update your details." The link may look like https://paypa1.com or use a misspelled domain.

How to Spot Fake Websites

  • URL mismatches: Check the address bar carefully for typos, extra words, or unusual domains.
  • HTTP vs HTTPS: HTTPS alone is not enough; phishing sites can use SSL too.
  • Unusual login prompts: If a site asks for more data than normal, stop and verify the source.
  • Branding inconsistencies: Missing logos, awkward layout, or low-quality images are warning signs.

Social Engineering Tactics

Attackers use human psychology to bypass security:

  • Authority: Pretending to be a boss, executive, or IT support.
  • Urgency: Forcing fast action by threatening deadlines or consequences.
  • Reciprocity: Offering help or a reward in exchange for information.
  • Scarcity: Claiming your account or package will be lost unless you respond quickly.
  • Familiarity: Using your name, company, or contacts to feel legitimate.
Tip: Always verify unusual requests by contacting the sender through a known, trusted channel — not by replying to the suspicious message.

Best Practices to Avoid Falling Victim

  • Hover over links before clicking to reveal the real destination.
  • Never enter credentials on a website opened from an email link unless you confirmed it is legitimate.
  • Use multi-factor authentication (MFA) whenever available.
  • Keep software and browsers up to date.
  • Report suspicious messages to your security team immediately.

Real-World Examples

Example 1: Invoice fraud

An attacker spoofs a supplier and sends an invoice with a new bank account. The email looks authoritative but the account details are incorrect.

Example 2: Password reset scam

"We detected unusual activity. Reset your password now." The link directs to a clone of a login page that steals your credentials.

Example 3: CEO impersonation

A message appears to come from leadership and asks for a rush wire transfer. The attacker relies on urgency and authority to bypass normal checks.

Interactive Quiz

Question 1

You receive an email from your bank asking you to verify your account by clicking a link. The message says your access will be suspended in 24 hours. What should you do?

Question 2

The URL of a login page is https://secure-login.example.co, but you expected example.com. What does this suggest?

Question 3

A colleague sends a chat message with a shortened URL and says, "Check this urgent file now." What is the safest first step?

Keep phishing awareness active: stay cautious, verify requests, and report suspicious emails.
