Recognize phishing emails

Red flags to watch for in emails and messages:

  • Urgency or fear: “Act now or your account will be closed.” Pressure bypasses careful thinking.
  • Mismatched sender: Display name says “Support” but the address is odd (e.g., support@compa ny-secure.net).
  • Suspicious links: Link text says one domain but the actual URL points elsewhere. Hover to preview.
  • Attachments you didn’t expect: Especially .html, .exe, .zip, .iso, or “invoice” out of the blue.
  • Grammar and tone issues: Unusual phrasing, inconsistent branding, or off greetings (“Dear user”).
  • Requests for credentials or MFA codes: Legitimate teams won’t ask you to share passwords or codes by email/SMS.
  • Too good to be true: Unexpected refunds, prizes, or job offers with prepayment.
How to safely inspect links
  • Hover over links on desktop to see the true destination in your status bar or tooltip.
  • On mobile, long-press to preview the URL. Check domain spelling and top-level domain (e.g., .com vs .co).
  • Type the known site address yourself in a new tab instead of clicking.
  • Use your company’s browser isolation or sandbox if available for suspicious links.

Spotting fake websites

Check the domain

  • Look for typosquatting: paypaI.com (capital i) vs paypal.com.
  • Beware of subdomain tricks: login.paypal.com.attacker.site is not PayPal.
  • Use official apps or bookmarks for sensitive logins.

Page signals

  • HTTPS padlock is necessary but not sufficient — attackers can get certificates too.
  • Design inconsistencies: off logos, colors, broken UI, odd language.
  • Form requests for recovery codes, MFA seeds, or full SSN are red flags.
Advanced: Validate with WHOIS and reputation checks
  • Newly created domains are riskier; many scams spin up within days.
  • Use reputation tools provided by your security team before entering data.

Social engineering tactics

Attackers manipulate trust, time, and curiosity. Common plays:

  • Authority: “I’m the CFO; wire funds now.” May include spoofed numbers or deepfake voice.
  • Urgency/Scarcity: “Offer expires in 30 minutes.”
  • Reciprocity: “I sent the doc; can you review and log in?”
  • Pretexting: Fake IT support ticket or HR notice to harvest credentials.
  • Tailoring: Spear phishing with details from LinkedIn or social media.
  • Multichannel: Email + SMS (smishing) + call (vishing) to reinforce legitimacy.
  • Deepfakes: Synthetic voice/video mimicking leaders to approve payments.
  • Collaboration app lures: Fake Teams/Slack/DocuSign/Drive notifications.
  • QR phishing (“quishing”): Stickers or images redirecting to credential traps.
    • Verification beats speed: independently confirm unusual requests via a known-good channel (e.g., call the person using a saved number).

    Best practices to avoid falling victim

    • Pause and verify: For any sensitive request, confirm through a separate channel.
    • Use the Report button in Outlook/Gmail or your company portal to alert security.
    • Hover and inspect links; never enter credentials after clicking unexpected links.
    • Keep MFA on and beware of fatigue attacks (multiple prompts). Deny unexpected prompts.
    • Update software and browsers to reduce exploit risk.
    • Unique, strong passwords + password manager. Enable passkeys when available.
    • When in doubt, don’t click. Ask security — false alarms are better than incidents.
